# Compensation Negotiation — Principal Engineer

## Understand the Comp Structure First

```
Total Compensation (TC) = Base Salary + Annual Bonus + Equity (annualized)

Example Principal Engineer TC:
  Base:   $180,000
  Bonus:  15% target = $27,000
  Equity: $400,000 RSUs over 4 years = $100,000/year
  TC:     ~$307,000/year

Always compare total comp, not just base.
Equity vesting schedules matter: 4-year cliff vs monthly vs annual.
```

### Equity Types
```
RSUs (Restricted Stock Units):
  - Shares granted; vest over time (typically 4yr, 1yr cliff)
  - Taxed as ordinary income when they vest (not when granted)
  - No exercise price — they're worth whatever the stock is worth at vest
  - Public company RSUs: liquid immediately on vest
  - Private company RSUs: illiquid until IPO/acquisition

Stock Options (ISOs / NSOs):
  - Right to buy shares at strike price (exercise price)
  - Value = current price - strike price
  - ISOs: favorable tax treatment (AMT risk for large grants)
  - NSOs: ordinary income on exercise spread
  - Private company: may need cash to exercise (risk: company never IPOs)

Phantom equity / SAR:
  - Common at private companies that don't want to issue real equity
  - Cash payment tied to company value — similar economics, no ownership

Ask any private company:
  - What is the current preferred price / 409A valuation?
  - What is the current preferred liquidation preference?
  - What % of the fully diluted cap table does my grant represent?
  - What was the last round valuation, and when was it?
```

## Leveling Conversations

At Principal level, leveling directly determines total comp. Fight for the right level.

```
The problem: companies often under-level to save money and "give you room to grow."
Under-leveling costs you: lower base, smaller equity grant, lower bonus target.

How to handle leveling conversations:

1. Know your target level before the loop starts.
   Ask the recruiter: "What level is this role mapped to? 
   Is there flexibility depending on the candidate's experience?"

2. Present evidence proactively.
   Don't wait for them to assess you — tell them your scope.
   "I want to make sure we're aligned on level. In my current role I [specific scope: 
   team size, system scale, org-level impact]. Based on that, I'd expect to be 
   evaluating at the [L7/Staff/Principal] level."

3. If they come back with a lower level:
   "I appreciate you sharing that. Can you help me understand what specific 
   experience or scope would have tipped the evaluation toward [target level]? 
   I want to make sure we're looking at the same data."

4. Level-up ask:
   "Given [specific evidence], would you be open to revisiting the level 
   with the hiring committee? I'm very interested in [company] and want 
   to make sure we find the right fit."

5. If they won't move on level:
   Ask about the top of the band for the level they're offering,
   and whether there's a faster path to promotion.
```

## The Negotiation Playbook

### Rule 1: Never Give a Number First
```
Recruiter: "What are your salary expectations?"

Options:
  A) Deflect: "I'm more focused on finding the right role and company fit. 
     I trust you'll make a competitive offer based on my experience. 
     What's the budgeted range for this position?"

  B) Flip it: "I've been doing some research but ranges vary a lot. 
     What's the range budgeted for this role?"

  C) If pressed hard: Give a wide range that starts higher than your target.
     "Based on my research, I'd expect something in the $X to $Y range for 
     total comp, but I'm flexible depending on the full package."

Why: Whoever gives the first number anchors the negotiation.
     If you say $200K and the budget was $280K, you've left $80K on the table.
```

### Rule 2: Never Accept on the Spot
```
When you receive an offer:
  "Thank you — I'm genuinely excited about this opportunity. 
   I'd like to take a few days to review the full details carefully. 
   When do you need my decision?"

Minimum: take 48-72 hours regardless.
If they pressure for same-day: "I want to make sure this is the right decision 
for both of us. Taking a couple of days to review thoroughly will help me 
start with full commitment. Can we say [date]?"
```

### Rule 3: Negotiate Everything at Once (Not Piecemeal)
```
Once you have the offer, come back with your full ask in one conversation:
  Base, equity refresh, signing bonus, start date, remote work, title — all at once.

NOT: "Can you increase the base?" → they move it → "Can you increase equity?" → etc.
     This feels adversarial and burns goodwill fast.

Instead:
  "I'm really excited about the role and team. I've reviewed the offer carefully 
   and there are a couple of things I'd like to discuss. On base, I was hoping for 
   [X] — that would put me at the midpoint for the Principal band at comparable 
   companies. And on equity, I'm looking for [Y] — I see [company] is at [stage] 
   and I want to make sure I have meaningful upside. Is there flexibility on either 
   or both of those?"
```

### Rule 4: Competing Offers Are Your Best Leverage
```
You don't need to accept competing offers — you just need them.

"I do have another offer I'm considering with a deadline of [date]. 
 I want to be transparent: [company] is my first choice, but I need 
 to be responsible about the decision. Is there anything you can do 
 to help me make this easier?"

This is honest, not manipulative, and often unlocks:
  - Faster decisions
  - Increased offers
  - More flexibility on non-salary items

Don't bluff — if they ask the company name, you should be able to confirm it.
Don't use fake competing offers. It destroys trust if discovered.
```

### Rule 5: Justify With Data, Not Emotion
```
Wrong: "I was hoping for more."
Right: "Based on Levels.fyi data for [role, location, company size], 
        the median TC is [X]. I'm looking for something closer to that."

Sources for market data:
  - Levels.fyi (most comprehensive for tech, especially equity data)
  - LinkedIn Salary
  - Glassdoor
  - Blind (anonymous but directionally useful)
  - Your network (ask peers at similar companies — less awkward than it sounds)
```

## What's Negotiable Beyond Base Salary

```
Signing bonus:
  - Often easier to increase than base (one-time cost, not recurring)
  - "My current company has unvested equity I'd be leaving on the table. 
     A signing bonus would help offset that."
  - Typically: 1-3 months of base salary at Principal level
  - Repayment clause: usually must repay if you leave within 1-2 years

Equity (RSU grant size):
  - Negotiate the initial grant size, not just base
  - At public companies: "Can we increase the RSU grant from $300K to $400K?"
  - Refreshers: ask about the annual refresh program — "How does equity refresh work here?"

Remote / location:
  - "I'd like to confirm that this role is [remote/hybrid] — 
     is there flexibility on the office requirements?"
  - Remote tier: some companies pay differently by location — confirm which tier

Start date:
  - More flexible than salary — often easier to get 2-3 extra weeks
  - Useful if you have unvested equity that vests soon

Title:
  - "Principal Software Engineer" vs "Staff Software Engineer" vs "Senior Principal"
  - Title affects external perception and often anchors your next offer
  - If they can't match salary, sometimes they can match title

PTO / sabbatical:
  - Many companies have unlimited PTO (which often means less vacation in practice)
  - Negotiate a minimum: "I'd like to confirm I can take at least [N] weeks per year"

Professional development:
  - Conference budget, L&D budget, paid certifications
  - Small dollar amount, easy for them to agree to, signal of investment in you
```

## Evaluating the Full Offer (Beyond Numbers)

```
Questions to ask before deciding:

1. "What does the equity refresh program look like? 
    Do you grant refreshes annually, and what's the typical amount at this level?"
    → Refreshes are often as valuable as the initial grant over time

2. "What's the current 409A valuation and preferred liquidation stack?" 
    (private companies only)
    → Determines if equity has any real value

3. "What's the path to the next level from here, and what does that 
    typically look like in terms of timeline and what's evaluated?"
    → Shows whether you can grow, or if it's a ceiling

4. "Is this role remote-friendly long term, or is there an expectation 
    of more in-office time as the company grows?"
    → Policy can change; get it in writing if it matters

5. "What's the vesting schedule and cliff?" 
    → Standard: 4yr/1yr cliff. Non-standard: proceed with caution.
```

## Offer Comparison Framework

```
Score each offer across dimensions (1-10):

| Dimension              | Offer A | Offer B |
|------------------------|---------|---------|
| Total comp (Year 1)    |         |         |
| Total comp (Year 4)    |         |         |
| Equity upside (risk)   |         |         |
| Role scope / level     |         |         |
| Technical challenge    |         |         |
| Team quality           |         |         |
| Manager quality        |         |         |
| Growth trajectory      |         |         |
| Work-life sustainability|        |         |
| Mission alignment      |         |         |
| Location / remote      |         |         |

Weight the dimensions by what matters to you most.
Don't choose purely on Year 1 TC — trajectory often matters more.
```

## Scripts for Common Negotiation Moments

**Receiving the offer:**
> "Thank you — I'm really excited. This is a role I've wanted and a team I'd be proud to join. I'd like to take a couple of days to review the full details carefully. When do you need my decision?"

**Counter-offer on base:**
> "I'm thrilled about the role and the team. On base, I was hoping for [$X] — I did some research on Levels.fyi for [similar roles] and that's closer to the midpoint for this level in [city/remote]. Is there flexibility to get closer to that?"

**Counter-offer on equity:**
> "The equity grant is important to me because I want to have meaningful skin in the game as [company] grows. I was hoping for [$Y] — would it be possible to increase the grant to that level?"

**Using a competing offer:**
> "I want to be transparent — I do have another offer I'm considering. [Company] is genuinely my first choice, and I'm hoping we can find something that makes this decision easy. Is there any flexibility on [base / equity / signing]?"

**Accepting:**
> "I'm really pleased to accept. I'm looking forward to joining the team. Can you send over the formal offer letter? And I'll plan to give my notice [Monday/tomorrow]."

**Declining gracefully:**
> "I've given this a lot of thought and ultimately decided to go a different direction. I have a lot of respect for [company] and the team — this was a genuinely difficult decision. I hope our paths cross again."
